Module 02 / 08 — Attack Taxonomy

Types of Phishing Attacks

Not all phishing looks the same. Attackers tailor their methods to the target and the medium.

📧
Email Phishing
Mass-distributed fraudulent emails impersonating trusted brands. The most common form — volume-based, cast a wide net.
high volumecommon
🎯
Spear Phishing
Targeted attacks using personalized information about the victim. Researched, precise, and far more dangerous.
targeteddangerous
🐋
Whaling
Spear phishing that specifically targets C-suite executives (CEO, CFO). Often involves wire fraud or credential theft.
executiveshigh-value
📱

SMS-based (smishing) and voice call-based (vishing) attacks. Exploits trust in phone communication.

A legitimate, previously received email is cloned with malicious links or attachments substituted in.

🚩 Key Red Flags Identified Misspelled sender domain · Generic greeting · False urgency · Artificial deadline · Suspicious URL with misleading "secure" keyword · No legitimate contact information
Module 04 / 08 — Fake Websites

Spotting Fake Websites

Phishing sites can look pixel-perfect. Here's how to catch them even when the page looks legitimate.

  • 🔒
    Check the URL, not just HTTPS — HTTPS means the connection is encrypted, NOT that the site is legitimate. Phishing sites also use SSL certificates.
  • 🔍
    Inspect the full domain name — Look for typosquats: paypa1.com, g00gle.com, amazon-secure.net. The subdomain is not the domain.
  • 📋
    Check the address bar before typing — Hover over links first. Your browser shows the true destination URL in the status bar.
  • 🏛️
    Verify with the real site independently — Type the known URL manually. Never follow links in emails for banking or sensitive logins.
  • 📅
    Check domain registration age — New domains (registered days/weeks ago) are a major red flag. Use WHOIS lookup tools.
⚠ HTTPS Misconception Over 90% of phishing sites now use HTTPS. A padlock icon does NOT mean the site is safe — it only means your connection to the (possibly malicious) site is encrypted.

URLScan.io — analyze website behavior
Google Safe Browsing — check site reputation

Module 05 / 08 — Social Engineering

The Psychology of Deception

Attackers exploit fundamental human psychology, not just technology. Understanding the six triggers helps you resist them.

⏱️
Urgency
"Act now or lose access!" — Panic bypasses rational thinking. Slow down when an email demands immediate action.
😨
Fear
"Your account has been compromised!" — Fear triggers fight-or-flight, not critical analysis. Question fear-based messages.
👑
Authority
Impersonating the CEO, IT department, or government agencies. Always verify unusual requests through a separate channel.
🤝
Reciprocity
"You've won a prize!" — Creates a sense of obligation. Unsolicited offers are almost always manipulation.
👥
Social Proof
"Millions of users have already updated their info." — Herd mentality pressure. Legitimate services don't pressure this way.
❤️
Liking
Pretending to be a colleague or friend to lower your guard. Attackers research social media to craft personalized lures.
💡 Golden Rule Any message that tries to make you feel rushed, scared, or obligated to click a link is almost certainly manipulation. Stop. Breathe. Verify independently.

Module 07 / 08 — Defence Playbook

Your Defence Playbook

Building these habits creates a strong personal security posture against phishing attacks.

  • 🔐
    Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) — Even if your credentials are phished, MFA blocks 99.9% of account takeover attacks. Use authenticator apps, not SMS.
  • 🔑
    Use a Password Manager — Unique, complex passwords for every account. If you reuse passwords, one phishing success unlocks everything.
  • 📧
    Verify Sender Identity Independently — If an email from your bank or boss seems unusual, call them directly using a known-good number. Never use contact info from the suspicious email itself.
  • 🔗
    Hover Before You Click — Always hover over links to preview the real destination URL. On mobile, long-press to inspect the link before tapping.
  • 📎
    Never Open Unexpected Attachments — Malicious macros, PDFs, and ZIP files are common delivery mechanisms. Confirm with the sender via a separate channel first.
  • 🛡️
    Keep Software Updated — Phishing often pairs with exploit kits that target unpatched browsers and OS vulnerabilities. Patches close those doors.
  • 🏢
    Report Suspicious Emails — Use your organisation's "Report Phishing" button or forward to your security team. Reporting protects everyone, not just you.
  • 🎓
    Stay Informed — Follow security news (Krebs on Security, The Hacker News). Attackers constantly evolve their tactics; your knowledge must too.
